The Core Insight

"I wrote down every kind of movement in my videos. There were only 4."

Before this rebuild, each video took 4–8 hours in After Effects: keyframing diagrams to appear, keyframing zooms on stills, lip-syncing characters, and picking transitions one at a time. Cap was 8 videos/month.

Then the creator wrote down every distinct kind of motion that ever appeared in their finished videos. The list was tiny:

  • Diagrams where lines / shapes appear in sequence
  • Still images that slowly zoom or pan
  • Character mouths that open and close while talking
  • Scene transitions between segments

That's it. So the project became: hand each of those 4 things to a deterministic pipeline (image-gen AI + Python + FFmpeg + sub-agents), and never touch AE again.

Principle 01
01

Diagrams that animate in sequence — split one image into ~60 line clusters

図の順番に出る · "zu no junban ni deru"
Before · After Effects
Place each shape on its own layer, set keyframes for when each one appears. ~5–10 sec per slide × 30 slides = several hours.
→
After · Code
Generate the slide as one PNG with AI, then let Python find ~60 connected line clusters in it. A sub-agent decides reading order, FFmpeg reveals them one by one.
1 PNG → ~60 clusters → ordered list → animated reveal · ~10 sec per slide

5 reveal styles the sub-agent picks from per cluster: stretch left→right, stretch top→bottom, pop-expand, bounce in, soft fade.

30 slides render in ~5 minutes at 6× parallel. The same job in AE used to take hours.

Mistake learned · once
Forgot to run the cluster-order conversion step before the sub-agent. The render finished happily — but the final video showed the full diagram, static, from frame one. Silent failure is the scariest kind. Added an automatic guard that re-runs the step if the order file isn't translated.
Principle 02
02

Still-image zoom & pan — one line of FFmpeg replaces 15 minutes of keyframing

静止画のより · "seishiga no yori"
Before · After Effects
Manually keyframe a zoom on every still — ~30 sec per image. 30 stills per video = 15 minutes of keyboard work, every single video.
→
After · FFmpeg zoompan
Add one line to the encode command. Long-form: 1.00 → 1.15 zoom over 2–5 sec. Vertical shorts: 1.00 → 1.06 (smaller screen, gentler push).
A barely-perceptible zoom — enough to read as "moving" without inducing motion sickness.

When a client asks "can you make that zoom a little faster?" the answer is: change one FFmpeg argument, rebuild in 5 minutes. No timeline to re-open.

Mistake learned · once
Once pushed the zoom to 1.00 → 1.30. Viewers complained of motion sickness. Re-watched it — yeah, way too aggressive. The rule now: use the minimum zoom that still reads as motion. 1.15 max for long-form.
Principle 03
03

Character lip-sync — don't listen to the audio. Switch mouths 7× per second, mechanically.

キャラの口パク · "chara no kuchipaku"
Before · After Effects
Listen to the narration, keyframe mouth open/close 5–8× per second. A 5-min video = ~1,500 manual keyframes. Impossible by hand.
→
After · One-line formula
Ignore the audio waveform entirely. Flip the mouth open/closed at a fixed 7 Hz. 5-min video = 2,100 switches, all from one expression.

The creator never opened the audio editor, never looked at a waveform. "Nobody has ever told me the mouth and audio don't match." 7 Hz happens to fall inside the natural cadence the human eye reads as "talking."

Expressions, same trick

4 expressions (joy / neutral / surprise / worry) × 2 mouth states = 8 PNGs per character. 3 characters × 8 = 24 image files total. The script just labels each line "joy" or "worry" and the right PNG gets pulled.

Mistake learned · once
Spun up 4 Voicebox engines in parallel for different channels. All 4 tried to write to the same pronunciation dictionary at once — dictionary corrupted, all 4 engines went silent. Now: 4 can read in parallel, only 1 may write at a time. Lock added.
Principle 04
04

Scene transitions — hexagonal shutters, two motions, nothing else.

場面切り替え · "bamen kirikae"
Before · After Effects
Pick a cross-dissolve / wipe / slide preset per scene, drop it on the timeline. 1–2 min per scene × 20–30 scenes = 30–60 min/video.
→
After · Two fixed motions
Hexagonal mask on character bust-ups (left/right of frame). Scene change = pop-in (0.35s). Expression change = soft cross-fade (0.4s). That's the entire transition library.
Bust-up = top 28% of the standing character art, masked to a hexagon. Two motions handle every cut.
Mistake learned · once
Used to add a constant ~4-second-cycle wobble to the masked windows to "feel lively." Viewers said it was distracting. Re-watched it — yep, eyes wouldn't settle. Killed the wobble. The pop + cross-fade alone give enough perceived motion; killing the wobble made the whiteboard easier to read.
For Anyone Still Editing in AE

You don't have to rip out your whole workflow.

If you're a part-time editor doing ~10 videos a month at 5 hours each, the creator's quick estimate: adopt just Principle 02 (FFmpeg zoom) and Principle 04 (hexagon wipes) and you save 1–2 hours per video. That's 10–20 hours back in your month without changing your whole pipeline.

The closing thought, paraphrased

"Eight months ago, every video cost me 4–8 hours of motion work. Now that work is one line of arguments per render. 8 videos a month became 240.
